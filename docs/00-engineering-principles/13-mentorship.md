# Mentorship

## Why this Principle Exists

Teams cannot scale judgment through documentation and review alone. Engineers need supported responsibility, timely feedback, and access to the reasoning behind decisions. Mentorship transfers capability without creating dependence.

## Philosophy

Mentorship is a structured relationship for improving the mentee's independent judgment. The mentor supplies context, questions, feedback, opportunity, and safety. The mentee owns goals and practice. Success is reduced dependence and increased scope handled responsibly.

## Core Ideas

- **Coaching:** Use questions to develop the mentee's model before prescribing a solution.
- **Feedback:** Make feedback specific, timely, behavior-based, and connected to impact.
- **Knowledge transfer:** Combine explanation with observation, practice, review, and teach-back.
- **Pair programming:** Use pairing for joint reasoning, not silent implementation by the senior participant.
- **Growth plans:** Define target capability, evidence, assignments, support, and review dates.
- **Progressive responsibility:** Increase ambiguity, blast radius, and stakeholder scope as evidence grows.
- **Psychological safety:** Make uncertainty and mistakes discussable while retaining accountability for learning and controls.

## Engineering Mindset

Diagnose whether the gap is context, knowledge, skill, confidence, opportunity, or feedback. Choose the smallest intervention that helps the engineer do the reasoning. Do not take over merely because execution would be faster in the moment.

## Real World Examples

1. **Design ownership:** Let the mentee frame drivers and options, review the reasoning before the solution, and assign them to present the decision.
2. **Incident participation:** Use a shadow, co-pilot, lead progression with explicit safety boundaries and a debrief.
3. **Review feedback:** Explain the system consequence, ask the mentee to propose a change, and later check whether the principle transfers.

## Common Mistakes

- Giving answers so quickly that the mentee never practices diagnosis or decision making.
- Assigning high-risk work without context, authority, review points, or a recovery path.
- Making vague personality feedback instead of describing behavior and impact.
- Creating a mentor dependency by keeping ownership and stakeholder access centralized.

## Trade-offs

| Tension                       | Practical position                                                                                             |
| ----------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Delivery speed vs development | Choose bounded tasks where learning value is high and failure is recoverable; keep critical support available. |
| Challenge vs safety           | Stretch one dimension at a time and define escalation conditions.                                              |
| Advice vs autonomy            | Offer context and constraints first; prescribe only when risk or missing foundations require it.               |

## Technical Lead Perspective

The lead makes mentoring a team system rather than a private favor. They match goals with real assignments, recognize mentoring work, rotate pairing and review opportunities, and watch for unequal access to sponsorship, context, or high-value work.

## Questions to Ask Yourself

- What capability should become independent, and what evidence will show it?
- Am I coaching the reasoning or demonstrating my own expertise?
- Does the assignment have enough authority, support, and safety?
- Is access to mentorship and consequential work distributed fairly?

## Checklist

- [ ] Goals, capability evidence, and review dates are agreed.
- [ ] Assignments provide real responsibility with bounded risk.
- [ ] Feedback is specific, timely, and actionable.
- [ ] The mentee explains and applies the reasoning independently.
- [ ] Progress reduces dependency and increases responsible scope.
